Designing IoT Solutions for Elderly Care: Harmonising Connection, Autonomy, and Secure Support

Sep 20, 2014

While leading research with stakeholders from Microsoft, Kent County Council, The Good Care Group, and UCL’s Departments of Computer Science and Brain Sciences, I’ve had the privilege to explore how IoT technologies can better serve our aging population. Rooted in rigorous research and countless conversations with individuals experiencing conditions like dementia, cancer, and other vulnerabilities, I’ve witnessed firsthand the deeply interwoven networks of care that sustain individuals. These experiences have profoundly shaped my conviction that effective elder care technology must honour the web of human relationships and shared autonomy that defines true support.

A Paradigm Shift in Elder Care: Weaving Technology into the Fabric of Human Connection

The journey toward impactful care technology starts with recognising its inherent relational structure. Care isn’t a singular, linear function; it’s a vibrant, multidimensional system where formal caregivers, family, and individuals coalesce. Yet, many existing systems are built with a reductionist approach, as if each person lives in isolation. This limits the transformative potential of IoT to merely transactional interactions. My research calls for us to look beyond the individual and develop IoT solutions that are symbiotic with the care ecosystems, harmonising with the values of trust, dignity, and independence each person desires.

Core Principles for Relationship-Centred, Autonomous Elder Care

Design for Relationships, Not Just Individuals

“User” in elder care is an expanded term—encompassing caregivers, family, and professionals. Imagine each person in this web as a distinct field of interaction. Whether it’s a son checking on his mother’s wellbeing from afar or a caregiver aligning medication schedules, the design must resonate with each unique relationship. Relationship-centred design thus recognises the powerful constellation of supporters around each person, dynamically interconnecting all roles in meaningful ways.

Enable Adaptive, Contextual Access

Just as relationships in a care network vary, IoT systems in elder care must respect fluidity in permissions. Each role requires specific access levels—family may need observational data, while caregivers interact more deeply. Enabling adaptable access honors these nuances, providing just the right visibility while maintaining privacy and agency.

Empower Autonomy Through the Principle of Consent

Consent is an essential force in the care spectrum. IoT solutions should allow for the modulation of permissions as needs evolve, allowing individuals to retain their agency. This consent-based architecture ensures users remain firmly in control of who can observe or interact with their data, preserving autonomy and fostering trust within a shared care framework.

Infuse Transparency to Sustain Trust and Security

Transparency acts like a stabilising force in the care matrix. For elderly individuals, having visibility into who accessed their information—and why—nurtures the grounding trust necessary for sustained, empowered use. Transparency becomes a vital aspect, offering reassurance to each participant in the network and ensuring they feel informed, protected, and respected.

Building a Resonant, Trustworthy Future for Elderly Care

This exploration of IoT in elder care reveals that technology is most powerful when it harmonises with the relational fabric of life itself. Grounded in the understanding that individuals are part of a living network, we can create tools that resonate with the dignity of independent yet interconnected lives. By designing technology that respects autonomy and transparency within the unique landscape of each person’s care ecosystem, we open doors to a future where IoT elevates the very essence of supportive, meaningful human connection.
